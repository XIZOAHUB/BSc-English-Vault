# 01: Word Accent and Stress Patterns

In English, we do not pronounce every syllable (vowel sound) in a word with the same force. We emphasize one specific part of a word to make it sound louder, longer, and at a higher pitch than the rest. This emphasis is called **Word Accent** or **Word Stress**.

Getting the stress wrong can make your speech difficult to understand or even change the meaning of the word entirely.

---

## 1. How is Stress Marked?
In dictionaries and the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA), primary stress is marked with a small vertical line (`ˈ`) placed **just before** the stressed syllable. 

*   **Example:** Teacher is transcribed as /ˈtiː.tʃər/. The mark before `tiː` shows that the first syllable is stressed.
*   *For easy reading in these notes, we will use **CAPITAL BOLD** letters to indicate the stressed syllable.*

---

## 2. The Golden Rule: Noun vs. Verb Shift (Heteronyms)
Many two-syllable words change their meaning and grammatical function depending on which syllable you stress. This is the most common stress pattern rule in the English language.

*   **Nouns and Adjectives:** Stress falls on the **FIRST** syllable.
*   **Verbs:** Stress falls on the **SECOND** syllable.

### Comparison Table: Noun vs. Verb Stress

| Word | As a Noun / Adjective (1st Syllable Stressed) | As a Verb (2nd Syllable Stressed) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Record** | **REC**-ord (I bought a new record) | re-**CORD** (Please record this audio) |
| **Present** | **PRE**-sent (I bought a birthday present) | pre-**SENT** (I will present my project) |
| **Object** | **OB**-ject (A flying object) | ob-**JECT** (I strongly object to this) |
| **Project** | **PROJ**-ect (My science project) | pro-**JECT** (Project the image on the wall) |
| **Conduct** | **CON**-duct (A strict code of conduct) | con-**DUCT** (They will conduct a survey) |
| **Desert** | **DES**-ert (The Sahara desert) | de-**SERT** (Do not desert me here) |
| **Export** | **EX**-port (Our main export is tea) | ex-**PORT** (We export goods to Europe) |

---

## 3. Stress Based on Suffixes (Word Endings)
The ending of a word (suffix) often dictates exactly where the stress will fall. 

### Rule A: Stress the syllable IMMEDIATELY BEFORE the suffix
Applies to words ending in: **-ic, -tion, -sion**
*   **-ic:** eco-**NOM**-ic, dra-**MAT**-ic, ro-**MAN**-tic
*   **-tion:** edu-**CA**-tion, in-for-**MA**-tion, con-tri-**BU**-tion
*   **-sion:** tele-**VI**-sion, de-**CI**-sion, ex-**PLO**-sion

### Rule B: Stress the THIRD syllable from the end (Antepenultimate)
Applies to words ending in: **-cy, -ty, -phy, -gy, -al**
*   **-cy:** de-**MOC**-ra-cy, po-**LICE**-cy
*   **-ty:** a-**BIL**-i-ty, un-cer-**TAIN**-ty
*   **-phy:** pho-**TOG**-ra-phy, ge-**OG**-ra-phy
*   **-gy:** bi-**OL**-o-gy, ge-**OL**-o-gy
*   **-al:** **CRIT**-i-cal, **LOG**-i-cal, ex-cep-**TION**-al

---

## 4. Compound Words
Compound words are made by joining two words together. Stress placement depends on the type of compound word.

*   **Compound Nouns (Stress the FIRST part):**
    *   **GREEN**-house
    *   **BLACK**-bird
    *   **FOOT**-ball
*   **Compound Verbs (Stress the SECOND part):**
    *   under-**STAND**
    *   over-**FLOW**
    *   out-**SMART**

---

> **💡 Quick Tip for Exams:** When asked to identify the stress pattern, always try reading the word aloud in a natural sentence rather than saying it in isolation. The rhythm of the sentence usually forces you to naturally stress the correct syllable.
